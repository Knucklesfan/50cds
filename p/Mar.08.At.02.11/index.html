The Royal Scam

I think everyone has something they'd call a "Desert Island" album. That one album that if you were forced to live on a beautiful deserted tropical island, left only with your local humble kerosene record player (which is not a very efficient device) and one record to your name, that'd be the one you'd pick. Something with tracks you'd never skip and songs you'd never forget.

Anyways, so here's The Royal Scam.

It's by far probably not my favorite record by Steely Dan, in fact depending on the day it might not even make my top ten. Yet there's someting about this album in particular that always has me coming back to it.

Maybe it's because it was my last Steely Dan album; I had owned their entire discography on CD for a good while before finally tracking down this one as my last. Maybe it's because of the darker, more dirty subject matter (although it's hard to get darker than Charlie Freak on Pretzel Logic), mixed with a very unique jazzy and darker tone compared to the rest of Steely Dan's discography. But maybe, just maybe, it's the lineup.

The thing about Steely Dan is that each of their albums bring a different sound. It seems like each time they're trying something new to reach that ultimate "perfection" that Donald Fagen and Walter Becker are notorious for trying to obtain, budget be damned. You can very easily tell if a track is from Katy Lied or Pretzel Logic, Gaucho or Aja, or even god forbid Can't Buy a Thrill versus literally anything else.

But in spite of this, Steely Dan has a very defined, very specific style. Even tracks released by Fagen as a solo artist after Steely Dan share this property, although more subdued. And even more to this effect, I think that Steely Dan itself has an issue with specific albums have tracks that are... just not great?

Every album by Steely Dan has at least one track that I usually would end up skipping when listening. Of course, if we're keeping with the kerosene record player desert island fantasy, then these are just the tracks you've gotta live with to get to the good stuff. But when listening to digitally, say, not on a desert island with a CD, these are the tracks that get skipped. Gonna go ahead and get my controversial list out of the way here, with a reason so that at least it's ont the end of the world.

  • Change Of The Guard - Can't Buy A Thrill
    • I know that this is a conversial take, but if given the chance I'd actually skip... three songs on Can't Buy a Thrill. It's just a bit too... normal? it's a great album, don't get me wrong, but very sonically and lyrically boring. It's very much a first album, but still has some great tracks in it.
  • The Boston Rag - Countdown to Ecstacy
    • Listen, I love this track as much as the next guy. Countdown to Ecstacy is actually probably a close second for "has no skippable songs", but the Boston Rag really actually gets better once the lyrics end. Not too sure what my problem with the lyrics are here, but they get on my nerves. But if you can stick with the song until around 2:45, that incredible 2 minute solo that plays out the song is fantastic.
  • Through With Buzz - Pretzel Logic
    • thank god this song is only 1:32 because if it was a second longer I would be through with this album every time I listen to it. Also, for it being 1:32, it feels so much longer than that. My problem with this track is by far the lyrics. AGONIZING. And it's right before Pretzel Logic too, which is likely my favorite track for that incredible bass.
  • Rose Darling - Katy Lied
    • A low point on a fantastic album! It's a decent track, and I do like it! Just, there's other tracks on Katy Lied that I consider to be significantly better.
  • Home At Last - Aja
    • This album has the same problem Katy Lied does, this is just a low point on generally what is the greatest album of all time. It's a fine song, but it's hard to beat every other track on Aja.
  • Third World Man - Gaucho
    • This track just sucks. The original lyrics were much better than the ones that actually were included in the final track, although clearly there they're pretty unfinished. Other than that, it's a fine ending to likely my favorite Steely Dan album, so I can't rag on it too much.

Okay, so with those thoughts out of the way, let's get back to business. The Royal Scam. There's just something about the track lineup and even track order that makes it just a joy to go through. Front and center, Kid Charlemagne is a great starting track. Spinning up Side A to immediately have the almost panicked, driving start of the track fills you with this dread that I think defines the entire album. It's just such a song about failure and fear about what's next. Starting the album with a track that effectively documents the death of the hippie movement sets a tone for the album: it's time to head back to the real world. Alongside this, Kid Charlemagne has this incredible guitar solo that few get even close to beating.

Straight out of Kid Charlemagne and right into The Caves of Altamira, which is another favorite track of mine from this album. The music sets this tone of a drift through your own memories, but weirdly enough, at least to me, it has an almost claustrophobic feeling. It's always meant to me this: the things that we experience now, the changes in the way that things seem to be, this feeling has always existed. We understood that these changes that we experience have always been things that humanity has experienced, so if we went through these changes back then and were okay, then why not now?

Don't Take Me Alive rocks. It just simply does. There's really very little I could say about this track, but to say that the "normal world" like Steely Dan describes through this album is inherently one that is uncomfortable and leads people to do drastic and dangerous things. Normal, average joes can snap at any time. Now suddenly you're holed up, hostages side by side, surrounded by police, and a case of dynamite. It's scary but fall in with the wrong people, make the wrong mistake, and you can find yourself in the wrong place at the wrong time, and it doesn't matter if you're a bookkeeper's son or not.

So maybe the way of snapping and falling in with the wrong people is the wrong approach. Or maybe you already found yourself making the mistakes and just need an easy way out. Well, won't you sign in, stranger? I think that this track describes exactly what it means to be one that falls for that Royal Scam of modern life, and falls in with a fast crowd. The entire song is about making yourself disappear, with the offering to become a "zombie" and sign your name on the dotted line and gain a new identity and disappear. You've already made your mistakes in life, so just go ahead and sign and suddenly all of those problems disappear. Now you're just another bum on the streets of a town far away. Right back at square one.

The Fez has such a dark and driving tone, combined with the repetitive and iconic lyrics makes for such a great track. I mean, seriously. listen to the chord progression in this track. Absolutely insane. A track like this really only can be found in The Royal Scam, and I think is a prime example of what I mean when I say that this album has a completely different sound.

I don't think I can say anything about Green Earrings that hasn't been said before. There is a reason why people say this is one of Steely Dan's greatest. It also sounds like you're listening to two songs at once, which usually would be something that I'd think would be unpleasant, but in the case of Green Earrings it works so well in it's favor that it's hard to believe that this track could exist without it. It's like napolean ice cream, and the switching melodies are a beautiful mixture of all three flavors.

Haitian Divorce is just such a great track, and yet another example of the Dan's skills at musical story-telling. It's generally an upsetting song, about a generally upsetting behavior. It plays directly into the story of modern life, especially at that time, where if divorce is hard to get, then run off to Haiti and get it done quick and easy! The entire point of the album is inherently that, the thing that may be the "easier" or more convenient or modern option results in a horrible ending; an ended marriage and an unexpected pregnancy.

Okay, so I'm gonna sound crazy. I actually theorize that Haitian Divorce and Everything You Did are two sides of the same coin. Haitian Divorce is from the wife's perspective, and Everything You Did is from the husband's perspective upon arrival back from Haiti. If so, jesus. The entire track is effectively a violent threat in the form of song. It's such a dirty and disgusting track, combined with the slower tempo and darker melody makes for a track that brings out some genuine disgust in me.

And lastly, we're left with The Royal Scam, and what a great finale. Steely Dan isn't always great at picking finale tracks. In particular, I think that Aja just kinda... ends? Josie is not a great finale for Aja, and could've honestly been placed anywhere else in the album and would've still been a great track. But boy, when Steely Dan makes a good ending pick, they make a great one. The Royal Scam is a fantastic song that sums up about everything that the album speaks on both narratively and musically. It's a fantastic track that feels from the start as though it's creeping out of the dark shadow of a skyscraper on a moon-less night. It's as smooth as butter, and has so many little things in the track that you only pick up on through later listens. It's such a fantastic ending to an already great album.

So, the final consensus. I don't know man. There's something about this album that just stands out to me from all of the other music by Steely Dan. Maybe it's the darker tone, maybe the chord progression or just the sheer fact that there are no songs in this album that I'd ever end up actually skipping.

Or maybe it's because you're never gonna do it without the fez on.

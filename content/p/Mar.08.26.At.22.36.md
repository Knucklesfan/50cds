This album is a lot to me. It may not be very much to you, and I completely understand that. It may not even be very much to many people. But to me, it's a lot. 

Al Stewart is such a weird artist to me. His music very frequently treads a line between being just [a little too sappy (I do like this song)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=keq8PDwOHco&pp=ygUXTW9kZXJuIFRpbWVzIGFsIHN0ZXdhcnQ%3D), and [sounding too much like a Ford Truck commercial at other times?](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DWfqMoC5dns) and yet, Year of the Cat has neither of these problems. 

How? Was it because it was produced by Alan Parsons. Yeah, THAT Alan Parsons. And to answer that question, no! Because Alan Parsons produced Modern Times and Time Passages as well. And both of those albums can't compete with Year of the Cat at all.

The fact that Year of the Cat turned out the way that it did is a mystery to me, and I'm sure there's a good reason for it. The title track alone is an album seller, but the entire album is FANTASTIC. It's strange, but for as alone at the end as Year of the Cat leaves me feeling, Lord Grenville fills me with this unspoken sadness.

It's a complete failure! They knew they were never going home. It's almost like that moment of peace and joy you feel right before something awful and horrible happens in your life. That last moment with your friends before school ended as a child. That one last date before the relationship ends. The nightmare before you awake to the real world.

Every track on here is either based on some true, historical story or a recounting of a vaguely fictional person. It's a very real and down-to-earth album that captures the triumphs and failures of humanity. Everyone can point at a track in this album and find something that they relate to on a sad, but personal way. 

Take Flying Sorcery. You want Amy to succeed so much. You're told of her childhood dream of flying, and now that she's finally gained it, you don't want the inevitable to finally catch up with her. And when she finally does fall to Icarus' fate, you feel bad for her, but know that she's still flying high in the sky. She'll fly forever, now.

It's just such an emotional album, and it's very hard to describe. I just recommend that you go on to listen to it. If you enjoy folksy, sappy soft rock, it's what you're looking for. But I think everyone could use to listen to this album at least once in their life. Turn off all the lights, close your eyes and listen. Visualize the world that Al Stewart describes. See it for yourself and come to your own conclusion. I don't think I could write a description of what it means to me without getting too personal.

It's that kind of personal album.
